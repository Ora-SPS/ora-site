RPE vs RIR: how to actually autoregulate.

RPE and RIR are two scales for the same thing — how close a set is to failure. Here’s the difference, the simple conversion, and how to use them to adjust training to the day instead of grinding a fixed number.

The short answer

RIR (reps in reserve) is how many reps you could still have done. RPE (rate of perceived exertion, on the 1–10 lifting scale) is roughly 10 − RIR. So RPE 8 means about 2 reps left, RPE 9 means 1, and RPE 10 means you hit failure. They’re the same idea — just pick one and use it consistently.

The conversion

  • RPE 10 — 0 reps in reserve (true failure).
  • RPE 9 — 1 rep in reserve.
  • RPE 8 — 2 reps in reserve.
  • RPE 7 — 3 reps in reserve.
  • RPE 6 or lower — 4+ reps in reserve (usually warm-up or speed work).

Why autoregulate at all

A fixed plan assumes every day is the same. They aren’t. Autoregulation means targeting an effort instead of a fixed load: “top set at RPE 8” lets the weight float up on good days and back off when you’re beat — so you get the intended stimulus without digging a recovery hole. It’s the same logic behind capping a set when fatigue is high.

How to use it

  1. Pick targets. e.g. main lifts at RPE 7–8, back-off sets at RPE 8, isolation closer to failure.
  2. Rate honestly, right after the set. Ask: how many clean reps did I have left?
  3. Adjust load to hit the target. If RPE 6 felt like the prescribed RPE 8, add weight; if you blew past it, take some off.
  4. Track it over time. Rising RPE at the same load is an early fatigue signal.

Caveat worth stating plainly: effort ratings are a skill. Most people — especially newer lifters — underestimate how hard a set was and stop with more in the tank than they think. Calibrate by rating the same lifts repeatedly and occasionally testing closer to failure.

FAQ

What’s the difference between RPE and RIR?
Same concept, two scales. RIR = reps left; RPE (1–10) ≈ 10 − RIR, so RPE 8 ≈ 2 RIR. Use one consistently.

What RPE is best for hypertrophy?
Most hard sets sit around 0–4 RIR (RPE 6–10); many lifters keep working sets near RPE 7–9 and push isolation closer to failure.

How accurate are the ratings?
They improve with experience and are more accurate near failure. Newer lifters tend to underestimate effort; rating over time calibrates it.
