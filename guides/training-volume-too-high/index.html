Is my training volume too high?

More sets help — until they don’t. Here’s how to spot when you’ve crossed from productive volume into junk volume, and how to fix it without throwing away progress.

The short answer

Volume is probably too high when adding sets stopped adding progress. Watch performance and recovery, not soreness alone: stalling or dropping loads and reps at the same effort, the same weights feeling harder than they should, and fatigue that compounds across the week. Past your personal recoverable volume, extra sets mostly add fatigue.

What “junk volume” actually is

Junk volume is work that adds fatigue without adding stimulus. Common culprits:

  • Sets stopped well short of failure (too many reps in reserve to count as hard).
  • Redundant exercises hammering the same muscle with little extra benefit.
  • Sets done so fatigued that form and range of motion fall apart.
  • Chasing a set number for its own sake instead of quality hard sets.

Signs you’re past your recoverable volume

  • Progress stalls or reverses. Estimated 1RM flat or dropping over several weeks.
  • Rising effort at the same load. Yesterday’s RPE 8 weights now feel like RPE 9–10.
  • Recovery debt. Persistent fatigue, poor sleep, nagging joints, lingering soreness.
  • Motivation drop. Sessions feel like a slog rather than work you can attack.

How to fix it

  1. Cut the low-quality sets first. Trim redundant or far-from-failure work, not your hardest, most productive sets.
  2. Make the rest count. Take working sets close to failure through a full range of motion.
  3. Deload if you’re run down. A lighter week often restores progress faster than pushing through.
  4. Rebuild gradually. Add a few sets at a time and confirm progress actually follows before adding more.

Caveat worth stating plainly: stalled progress isn’t always volume. Sleep, nutrition, stress, and program structure all matter. Rule those out before assuming you simply need to train less.

FAQ

How do I know my volume is too high?
Loads/reps stall at the same effort, the same weights feel harder, and fatigue builds week to week — especially if adding sets stopped adding progress.

What is junk volume?
Sets that add fatigue without stimulus — too far from failure, redundant, or too fatigued to be quality.

Should I just do less?
Do better first: cut low-quality sets, make the rest hard, deload if needed, then rebuild gradually.
